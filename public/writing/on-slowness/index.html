On Slowness

What we lose when we stop taking the long way home

There is a road I used to take that added twelve minutes to my commute. It wound past a field that changed with every season — stubble in autumn, a brilliant sheet of green in spring, and in summer a kind of gold so pure it seemed borrowed from somewhere else.

I stopped taking it somewhere around the third year. The twelve minutes began to feel like theft. I had things to do, messages to answer, a future to arrive at.

What I didn’t understand then is that the twelve minutes were the thing. The field was the point.

The Economy of Attention

We have built an entire civilization on the premise that faster is better — that the mind’s highest aspiration is to compress time, to close the gap between now and what comes next. But there are kinds of knowledge that require a certain pace to be received. You cannot rush a slow understanding.

The Japanese have a word, ma — the pause, the negative space, the breath between notes that makes music possible. We have filled all our ma. We have optimized ourselves out of our own experience.

I think about this when I read. There are books I have consumed and books I have inhabited. The difference is usually time.
